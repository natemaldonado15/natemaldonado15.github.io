> **Role:** Research Engineer, Saint-Gobain Abrasives  
> **Project period:** 2022 to 2024  
> **Public scope:** High-level only. Machine settings, supplier documentation, customer data, and proprietary operating logic are excluded.

## What

Polymer additive-manufacturing post-processing can drift into trial-and-error when part geometry, chamber conditions, solvent amount, and process duration interact in ways that are not obvious to an operator. This project focused on moving depowdering and chemical vapor smoothing from recipe-by-recipe adjustment toward a more repeatable, parameter-driven process.

Chemical vapor smoothing uses controlled solvent-vapor exposure to reflow the outer surface of polymer printed parts, improving surface quality and appearance. The engineering problem was to identify which inputs actually drove meaningful outcomes and to turn that knowledge into practical guidance for machine users.

## How

### Structured Experimentation

I designed experiments around machine and part inputs to identify the process drivers and interactions that most affected the output condition of printed parts. The work mapped:

- Part geometry and thickness effects
- Chamber and solvent inputs
- Process duration
- Measurable output qualities, including surface condition and process completion

JMP was used to organize factors, analyze trends, and convert trial results into a more systematic process map.

### Hardware and Guidance Improvements

The results informed upgrades to the process hardware and automated operating guidance. I also trained application engineers to use the updated workflow and support the technology in customer-facing work.

## Results

- Replaced a purely trial-and-error approach with a structured, parameter-driven understanding of the process.
- Delivered hardware and operating-guidance improvements that made the system easier to use and more efficient.
- Contributed to approximately **$325K in sales within six months** through improved commercialization and technical support.

## Validation and Engineering Lessons

| Engineering question | Evidence used | Outcome |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Which inputs drive post-processing response? | Designed experiments across machine and part factors | Identified significant process drivers and interactions |
| Can the operation be more repeatable? | Parameter mapping and operator workflow updates | Reduced dependence on informal recipe adjustment |
| Can the results be turned into usable guidance? | Hardware changes and automated process guidance | Improved ease of use for application engineers and customers |
| Does the improvement have business value? | Commercial adoption and sales contribution | Connected engineering work to market impact |

The core lesson was that post-processing performance depends on the interaction between part geometry, machine settings, and user workflow. Treating those factors as isolated knobs produces inconsistent outcomes. A structured DOE approach turns them into a usable process model.
