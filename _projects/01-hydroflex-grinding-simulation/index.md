# Problem

HydroFlex is a hydrodynamic flexible spindle polishing process developed at WPI for finishing internal channels in additively manufactured metal components. The process uses a small electroplated abrasive grinding wheel mounted on a flexible spindle and driven at high rotational speed inside an as-built channel.

Unlike a conventional prescribed-depth grinding model, HydroFlex material removal depends on the coupled motion of the grinding wheel, the local wheel-workpiece engagement, and the orbital motion of the wheel about the channel axis. That orbital behavior is influenced by three primary force mechanisms:

- Grinding forces between the wheel and workpiece
- Hydrodynamic forces generated by coolant flow around the rotating wheel
- Centrifugal force from the high-speed rotating wheel

My objective is to improve the understanding of how key HydroFlex inputs influence material removal and surface-finish evolution in L-PBF AlSi10Mg internal channels.

# Approach

## Wheel and Workpiece Representation

I characterized representative abrasive-wheel and workpiece surfaces using microscopy-derived topography and statistical analysis. I then used those surface data to construct a rigid finite-element abrasive wheel section and a deformable SPH representation of the L-PBF AlSi10Mg workpiece.

This approach is intended to preserve the local roughness needed for micron-scale contact behavior while keeping the contact region small enough for practical computation.

## Nonlinear FEM-SPH Model

The LS-DYNA model represents normal engagement as a force-controlled interaction rather than a prescribed depth of cut. Tangential motion is applied to represent local sliding between the abrasive wheel and workpiece surface. This setup is designed to evaluate how applied load, surface topography, and sliding conditions influence contact response and material removal.

The post-processing workflow extracts the signals needed to evaluate a force-controlled polishing process:

1. Contact-force history
2. Normal displacement and engagement behavior
3. Stress response and local deformation
4. Material removal and chip-like SPH separation
5. Surface-profile and roughness evolution

# Results

The current framework provides a method for connecting input conditions such as wheel rotational speed, channel-to-wheel diameter ratio, abrasive grit size, and measured surface topography to local contact response, material-removal behavior, and surface-roughness evolution.

The active validation plan compares model outputs with laboratory surface-roughness measurements from polished internal channels. The value of the project is the modeling workflow itself: it combines measured wheel and workpiece surface topographies with a force-controlled FEM-SPH contact model, rather than relying on a prescribed-displacement single-grit scratch simulation to represent HydroFlex physics.
